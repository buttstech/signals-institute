# **I. Institutional Identity**

**Mission:** The Signals Institute transforms underserved communities and addresses critical workforce gaps in advanced technology and engineering sectors through early youth exposure, structured veteran transition pathways, and rigorous, hands-on technical training—creating direct pathways to research and engineering careers while strengthening the depth, proficiency, and long-term resilience of the national workforce.

The Signals Institute is the execution layer of the Anne Huntington Foundation—where philosophy becomes human capability. It builds workforce, develops technical competence, and proves the practical value of community-integrated defense work through operational excellence.

"Signals" is plural by necessity. An institute belongs to a domain, not an instance. The plural reflects fields, systems, and distributed capability. The name requires no acronym or explanation.

# **II. The Three Pillars**

These pillars represent the Institute's primary avenues of engagement and contribution. They define who we serve and how we build capability at each level.

## **Pillar 1: Youth Exposure & Career Development**

Young people are the long-term foundation of technical workforce capacity. This pillar serves youth ages 10-18 through two distinct tracks calibrated to developmental stage and readiness.

**Track A — Exposure (Ages 10-14)**

Early exposure creates cultural familiarity where research and technical work become natural extensions of community capability. By demystifying scientific reasoning and advanced technology, we transform these fields from something distant and mysterious into something attainable and expected. We recruit researchers and develop suitable presentation agendas on their behalf, bringing working scientists directly into schools and community spaces. The goal is inspiration—making advanced technical work visible to young people who might otherwise never encounter it.

**Track B — Career Development (Ages 14-18)**

High school students are ready for structured skill-building, internship placement, and direct career preparation. This track moves beyond exposure into tangible workforce readiness: hands-on technical training, after-school programs, science fair mentorship, and internship pipelines connected to defense and technology employers. Participants build portfolios, earn certifications, and graduate with a clear pathway into college or direct technical employment.

## **Pillar 2: Veteran Transition**

Military personnel transitioning to civilian careers represent the highest-leverage population for building national technical capability. Veterans bring security clearances, technical foundations, disciplined work habits, and mission orientation. The Institute translates military signal experience into civilian capability and connects veterans directly to defense industry employment.

This pillar serves separating military personnel, veterans within 180 days of separation (SkillBridge eligible), and veterans seeking to leverage military experience in civilian technical careers.

## **Pillar 3: Community Engagement**

Community engagement extends our mission beyond direct programming to build lasting relationships with local organizations, schools, and civic groups. We create pathways for community members to connect with researchers, access technical resources, and participate in the broader ecosystem of technical education.

This pillar serves the broader community through:
- **Community Workshops**: Hosted at schools and community centers bringing researchers and research topics directly to the public
- **Technical Tool Adoption**: Workshops and resources for general technical skill-building accessible to all community members
- **Researcher Forums**: Open Q&A sessions where community members can engage with researchers, ask questions, and learn about ongoing work

**Curriculum Philosophy: Activity Library**

The Institute maintains a living curriculum library — a growing collection of hands-on activities, each designed to surface a concept through doing rather than instruction. Activities are the vehicle; concepts are the destination. A student builds a wire antenna, watches it pick up a signal on a screen, and asks why — the concept of resonance emerges from what they just experienced, not from a slide.

The library is built on Dr. Butts' technical background in RF, signals, and applied engineering, translated into age-appropriate activities using analogy and physical demonstration rather than theory. Younger students engage with the same real concepts as working engineers — just through a different door.

**Analogy as pedagogy:** Abstract concepts get concrete anchors before any technical language is introduced. An antenna is "an ear for invisible waves" before it is a radiating element. Signal classification is "teaching a computer the difference between a whisper and a shout" before it is a machine learning problem. The analogy is not a simplification — it is the accurate entry point.

The library is dynamic. Every researcher who comes through may generate a new activity. Every session that works well gets documented and added. The curriculum grows with the Institute's network.

**Sample Activity Library Entries**

| Activity | Concept Surfaced | Researcher Domain Pairing | Age Group |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Build a wire dipole, observe signal on SDR display | Antennas, resonance, invisible waves | RF engineers, physicists, astronomers | 10-14 |

**Sample Session: "How Does Your Phone Know Where You Are?"**
*Drawn from Dr. Butts' background in wireless signal capture and distributed RF systems*

- Analogy: your phone is always listening for invisible signals — like holding up an ear in a room full of whispers
- Activity: use an SDR dongle to visualize live RF signals in the room on a laptop screen — students see the "invisible" become visible
- Observation: identify FM radio, WiFi, and Bluetooth signals by frequency band — students label what each one is used for
- Discussion: "Who sends these signals? Who captures them? Who decides what they mean?" — opens into Dr. Butts' patent on distributed wireless signal capture
- Take-home: hand-drawn "signal map" of their home — what signals exist there, what devices send and receive them

**Sample Session: "How Do Drones Talk to Each Other?"**
*Drawn from Dr. Butts' DOD work on drone swarm coordination and distributed systems*

- Analogy: a flock of birds has no leader — they follow simple rules and move as one; drone swarms work the same way
- Activity: students "become" drones — each given a rule card (stay X feet from neighbor, face the same direction); group walks and self-organizes without a leader
- Demo: short video of actual drone swarm behavior; students identify the rules they just acted out
- Discussion: "What happens if one drone gets a bad signal? What if it gets the wrong instruction?" — opens into signal integrity and security
- Take-home: challenge card — draw a 5-drone swarm and write two rules that would make it work

**Sample Session: "What Is Radar and Can It See You?"**
*Drawn from Dr. Butts' work in radar design and RF/microwave systems*

- Analogy: radar is like shouting in a cave and listening for the echo — except the "shout" is a radio wave and the "echo" tells you exactly how far away something is
- Activity: ultrasonic rangefinder demo — students point a sensor at objects and read distances on a screen; they are operating a simple radar
- Observation: compare short-range (ultrasonic) vs. long-range (microwave) — why does range matter?
- Discussion: "Where is radar used that you didn't know about?" — weather, airports, cars, military — opens into Dr. Butts' radar design work
- Take-home: paper design challenge — "design a radar system for one of these problems" (find a lost hiker, track a storm, land a plane in fog)

## **2. Veteran Transition Plan**

### **Philosophy: Custom Pathways, Not One-Size-Fits-All**

No two veterans arrive with the same background. A communications specialist, a signals intelligence analyst, and an aircraft avionics technician each bring different technical foundations, clearance levels, and career goals. The Institute does not place veterans into fixed programs — we conduct an intake assessment and build an individualized transition pathway drawing from our available content areas. Tracks are menus, not mandates. A veteran may complete modules across multiple tracks, move at an accelerated pace through areas they already know, or spend extended time in areas where they need to build from scratch.

### **Facilitator Model**

The Institute does not rely on a single subject matter expert. We recruit and coordinate a network of qualified facilitators including:

- **Retired military professionals** with signals, communications, or intelligence backgrounds who understand the veteran experience firsthand
- **Defense industry practitioners** from contractors such as Northrop Grumman, L3Harris, Leidos, and SAIC who bring current hiring context and technical depth
- **University faculty** from FAMU-FSU College of Engineering, Florida Atlantic University, and similar institutions who can deliver technical curriculum with academic rigor
- **Career transition coaches** specializing in military-to-civilian transition, federal resume writing, and security clearance career navigation
- **AI/software professionals** who can deliver LLM, Python, and data tools training

This model allows the Institute to scale without bottlenecking through any one individual, and ensures veterans receive instruction from people with directly relevant industry experience.

**Outreach Method**

Outreach targets the veteran where they already are — on base, at the VA, and through trusted peer networks. Concrete outreach actions include:

- **TAP Office Partnerships**: Establish formal presenter relationships with Transition Assistance Program offices at MacDill Air Force Base (Tampa), Patrick Space Force Base (Cocoa Beach), and NAS Jacksonville — the three largest military installations within 250 miles of Riviera Beach
- **VA Medical Centers**: Partner with the West Palm Beach VA Medical Center and its community-based outpatient clinics to reach veterans already engaged with VA services
- **VSO Chapters**: Direct outreach to American Legion Post 160 (Riviera Beach), VFW Post 4143 (West Palm Beach), and Disabled American Veterans (DAV) Palm Beach County chapter for referrals and co-hosted information events
- **SkillBridge Registration**: Complete DOD SkillBridge Industry Partner registration to appear in the official SkillBridge directory, making the Institute discoverable by service members actively searching for approved programs
- **Defense Contractor HR Referrals**: Build relationships with HR and talent acquisition teams at L3Harris Technologies (Melbourne, FL headquarters), Northrop Grumman Florida operations, and DRS Technologies to receive veteran referrals and co-develop job placement pipelines
- **LinkedIn for Veterans**: Run targeted LinkedIn outreach and sponsored content campaigns reaching veterans in Palm Beach, Broward, and Martin counties with military experience in signals, communications, avionics, and intelligence

**Event Establishment**

- Information sessions hosted quarterly at MacDill AFB, Patrick SFB, and West Palm Beach VA Medical Center, and monthly virtually
- Intake process: online application → skills and background assessment interview → individualized pathway design meeting with Institute staff
- SkillBridge coordination: Institute staff manage all paperwork and base education office communication on behalf of the veteran
- Employer partner roundtables: biannual convenings with hiring partners to align curriculum with current hiring needs and confirm placement pipelines

**Sample Session: "Your Military Experience Has a Civilian Name — Let's Find It"**
*Professional Transition module — MOS/AFSC translation and job search tools*

- Open with reality check: most job postings don't say "signals intelligence" or "avionics tech" — but the skills transfer exactly; the language just needs translating
- Activity: each veteran looks up their MOS/AFSC on a civilian crosswalk tool (O*NET, MyNextMove for Vets) — identifies top 3 matching civilian job titles
- Walk through USAJOBS.gov together — create or update a profile, understand federal resume formatting vs. civilian resume, identify which fields map to military service records
- LinkedIn setup or audit — optimize headline and summary using defense-industry keywords; connect with Institute employer partners directly
- Activity: use Claude or ChatGPT to rewrite one bullet point from their military record into civilian language — compare before and after
- Close: each veteran leaves with a target job title, a civilian-translated resume bullet, and a LinkedIn profile updated or created

**Sample Session: "Using AI Tools to Get the Job — and Do the Job"**
*AI Documentation Tools module — LLM usage for professional transition*

- Framing: LLMs are not a shortcut — they are a force multiplier; veterans already know how to use tools under pressure, this is one more tool
- Demo: use Claude to draft a cover letter from scratch using just a job posting and a brief military background description — show the output, then improve it together
- Activity: veterans paste their own job posting into Claude and ask it to identify the top 5 skills the employer is looking for — then cross-reference against their own background
- Demo: use ChatGPT to generate technical documentation from a plain-language description — relevant for veterans moving into RF, systems, or defense tech roles
- Activity: each veteran uses an LLM to generate 5 interview questions for their target role, then practices answering one out loud
- Tool overview: Claude for writing and analysis, Perplexity for research, GitHub Copilot for code — when to use each
- Close: each veteran has a drafted cover letter and a list of interview questions tailored to their target role

**Sample Session: "RF and Signals — From Military to Civilian"**
*RF, Signals & Defense Technology module — grounded in Dr. Butts' expertise*

- Framing: if you worked with any radio, radar, communications, or electronic warfare system in the military, you already have the foundation — we're translating it to civilian engineering language
- Review: common military signal systems (SINCGARS, JTRS, Link 16, AESA radar) and their civilian equivalents (cellular, WiFi, commercial radar, spectrum management)
- Demo: SDR receiver capturing live spectrum — veterans identify familiar frequency bands from military experience; bridge from military context to civilian engineering terminology
- Discussion: clearance advantage — defense contractors actively seek candidates who already understand these systems; how to position military experience as technical depth, not just service history
- Intro to HFSS/CST simulation tools — the same software used at Northrop Grumman and L3Harris; veterans see that the technology is familiar, the software is learnable
- Close: each veteran maps one military system they worked with to a civilian job role that uses equivalent technology

## **3. Community Engagement Plan**

### **Facilitator Model**

Community engagement events are not lectures — they are facilitated dialogues. The Institute recruits facilitators who can hold space for honest, accessible conversation:

- **Active researchers** from any scientific domain who are willing to speak plainly about their work and answer unscripted questions
- **Community liaisons** with existing trust relationships in Riviera Beach and surrounding neighborhoods who help frame topics in locally relevant terms
- **Technical educators** who can lead hands-on tool adoption workshops for adults with no prior background
- **Civic moderators** — individuals skilled at facilitating open dialogue, managing divergent perspectives, and keeping conversations productive

- Technical Tool Adoption Sessions: Self-paced online resources on the Institute website paired with monthly in-person drop-in support sessions at the library

**Sample Forum: "What Is Defense Research and Why Does It Happen Here?"**
*Dialogue & Transparency — drawn from Dr. Butts' defense and community background*

- Who is in the room: open to all, no technical background assumed
- Opening: Dr. Butts shares his own story — growing up, FAMU-FSU, Northrop Grumman, Special Operations, back to Riviera Beach — why here, why this
- Plain-language explanation of what defense research actually is: not just weapons, but communications, navigation, radar, drones, cybersecurity — most of it is infrastructure
- "What does this have to do with our neighborhood?" — discussion of defense employment as an economic pipeline; who gets hired, who gets left out, and why this Institute exists to change that
- Open Q&A: moderated, all questions welcome — no topic is off-limits
- Close: what's coming next, how to get involved, how to refer a young person or veteran

**Sample Forum: "Signals Are Everywhere — What That Means for You"**
*Dialogue & Transparency / Technical Tool Adoption — grounded in Dr. Butts' RF and wireless research*

- Opening demo: handheld spectrum analyzer or SDR display showing live signals in the room — WiFi, cellular, Bluetooth, FM radio all visible simultaneously
- Plain-language explanation: every device that communicates wirelessly is sending a signal; those signals can be captured, analyzed, and identified
- Discussion: privacy, security, and the difference between a signal being detectable and being readable — demystifying what "they can see your phone" actually means technically
- Community relevance: smart city infrastructure, public safety communications, emergency alerting systems — signals that affect everyone
- Hands-on: attendees download a free spectrum viewer app on their phones and look at signals in the room together
- Open Q&A — moderator ensures all questions get addressed, not just technical ones

**Sample Forum: "How AI Is Changing Technical Work — And What That Means for Jobs"**
*Technical Tool Adoption — grounded in Dr. Butts' AI/ML and LLM expertise*

- Framing: AI tools are not replacing engineers — they are changing what engineers spend their time on; this matters for anyone thinking about a technical career
- Live demo: use Claude to answer a technical question, draft a document, and explain a complex concept in plain language — audience sees it in real time
- Discussion: what kinds of jobs are most affected, what kinds of skills become more valuable (judgment, communication, problem framing), what the Institute teaches and why
- Hands-on: attendees try a prompt on their own phones — ask an LLM one question relevant to their life or work
- Career Q&A: what does it take to get into a technical field from this community? What does the Institute offer? How do you get started?

## **4. Signals Youth Research Journal**

The *Signals Youth Research Journal* is an Institute-wide publication — not the output of any single program or pillar. It spans Youth and Community Engagement, giving young people a real research artifact and giving the broader community a visible record of what the Institute produces.

- **Authors**: High school students from the Career Development track, Science Fair Mentorship, Summer Technical Intensive, and community-connected youth participants
- **Content**: Project write-ups, technical explanations, research summaries, and investigative essays — written for a general scientific audience, any domain
- **Mentorship**: Each submission is paired with a researcher or faculty mentor who guides the student through drafting and revision
- **Publication cadence**: Spring and Fall issues; submissions accepted on a rolling basis
- **Distribution**: Institute website, partner schools, participating university libraries, employer partners, and community forum attendees
